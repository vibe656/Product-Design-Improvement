01 — Opportunity

Unlocking growth through localized payments

Noon is the MENA region's leading e-commerce platform — well-positioned, fast-growing, and serving millions across the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt. This case study explores how layering a localized payment option on top of an already strong checkout experience can expand reach and unlock incremental conversion from a high-value, underserved customer segment: international visitors and the cross-border gifting market.

The UAE consistently ranks among the world's top tourist destinations, with millions of visitors arriving each year. The visitor mix creates a clear opportunity to tailor payment options to user origin — without disrupting the smooth checkout flow that already works for Noon's core domestic shopper.

02 — Research & Insights

Understanding who's at the checkout

Dubai's tourism data tells a clear story. India is the city's #1 visitor source market — followed by Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, and Russia. These four markets together account for a significant share of international footfall and represent a meaningful slice of cross-border e-commerce demand.

UPI, India's real-time payment network, is already live in the UAE — not as a future possibility, but as working infrastructure. Since launching in the UAE in 2021, UPI has expanded through the NPCI International–AANI interlink (AANI being the UAE's own domestic instant-payments rail), with acquirers like Network International, Mashreq NeoPay, and Magnati already enabling QR-based UPI acceptance at over 60,000 merchants, including Dubai Duty Free and Lulu Hypermarket. The infrastructure exists; the user behaviour exists. The opportunity is to connect them at Noon's checkout — making it easier for a large Indian visitor base to transact, and unlocking a cross-border use case where Indians in India can order gifts for family in the UAE using their familiar payment rail.

03 — Proposed Solution

Adding UPI as a payment option for relevant users

Rather than overhauling the checkout for everyone, the proposal is targeted: introduce UPI as an additional payment option, surfaced only for users who are likely to value it. The existing checkout flow remains unchanged for everyone else.

When UPI is shown at checkout
🇮🇳 Indian phone number
→
UPI shown + existing methods
🇦🇪 UAE phone number
→
UPI shown + existing methods
🌍 Other origin
→
Existing methods only

This covers four real scenarios with a single rule: Indian phone numbers (whether logging in from the UAE or India) and UAE phone numbers (whether in the UAE or in India) both see UPI as an option. The first group includes Indian tourists in the UAE and Indian residents ordering gifts for family in the UAE. The second includes UAE residents who travel to India often, who have NRI family ties, or who simply prefer UPI for cross-border purchases. Users from other origins continue with the current checkout, unchanged.

Noon would integrate via a UAE acquirer that already supports UPI acceptance — such as Network International or Mashreq NeoPay — both of which connect to the NPCI International–AANI interlink. This means Noon isn't pioneering new cross-border rails; it's plugging into acquiring infrastructure that already serves the UAE market, which keeps the scope of change manageable against the potential upside.

05 — Validation Plan

A/B testing to de-risk the rollout

A structured A/B test would compare UPI-enabled flows against the existing checkout for Indian and UAE phone-number users. Success would be measured across three primary metrics: payment completion rate, drop-off at the payment step, and overall checkout-to-purchase conversion.

Variant A — UPI Enabled

  • UPI surfaced as primary option for Indian users
  • Familiar payment rail reduces cognitive friction
  • Tracks: conversion rate, payment success %
  • Tracks: drop-off at payment step

Variant B — Control

  • Existing checkout flow, unchanged
  • Cards / generic options presented first
  • Tracks: same metrics as Variant A
  • Baseline for all comparisons

How Noon's payment costs compare today

UPI's advantage isn't that it generates new revenue — it's that it costs less to accept than Noon's existing payment mix. The table below puts UPI in context against the methods Noon already supports.

Payment MethodTypical Merchant Cost
Cards (Visa / Mastercard) ~2–3% MDR
Apple Pay ~2–3% (same as underlying card — no separate Apple fee)
BNPL (Tabby / Tamara) ~5–7%+ (provider absorbs credit risk)
Cash on Delivery No fee, but 8–15% rejection rate drives logistics cost
UPI (bank transfer) ~0% MDR + flat gateway/platform fee
Figures are industry-typical ranges, not Noon-specific or confirmed contractual rates — final terms depend on Noon's existing processor agreements.

Where Noon's money actually comes from

A natural question: if UPI charges close to 0% MDR, how does Noon make money on a UPI-paid order? The answer is that Noon's revenue model doesn't change — only the cost of accepting payment does.

1

Product margin stays the same. Noon earns money the way it always does — the markup between what it pays for inventory and what the customer pays — regardless of payment method.

2

Settlement still lands in AED. The customer's UPI app debits INR from their Indian bank account, but the AANI interlink settles the transaction to Noon in AED — Noon never holds or manages INR directly.

3

The FX spread belongs to the payment rail, not Noon. Whoever sits in the INR→AED conversion (NPCI International, the acquiring bank, or AANI) typically earns a small currency-conversion margin — similar to how card networks earn FX markup on cross-border transactions. Noon doesn't capture this, but doesn't lose from it either.

4

The real lever is cost avoidance, not new revenue. Every UPI transaction saves Noon roughly the 2–3 percentage points it would otherwise pay in card MDR. On a high-volume, low-margin retail business, that falls straight to the bottom line as recovered margin rather than top-line growth.

This breakdown reasons from how cross-border QR-based instant-payment rails typically structure settlement and FX, based on publicly available descriptions of the UPI–AANI interlink. Exact commercial terms for a UAE merchant would need confirmation from Noon's treasury/payments team before being used in a live business case.

Localized payment strategies aren't a feature — they're a prerequisite for global e-commerce success.

07 — Key Takeaways

What this project demonstrates

This case study sits at the intersection of user research, payment infrastructure, and data-driven product thinking. The insight that drives the entire solution — Indian tourists are Noon's largest foreign segment — isn't a technical discovery. It's a customer empathy discovery that unlocks a technical opportunity.

The ability to recognize a demographic insight, translate it into a product hypothesis, design a testable rollout strategy, and quantify the expected business impact is exactly the kind of end-to-end thinking that moves metrics in e-commerce.

Applicable across any global platform where user origin diversity creates an untapped opportunity for localized experiences — and where small, targeted additions can unlock incremental growth without disrupting what already works.
